      \begin{block}{Abstract}

        The Power Play in mixed doubles curling allows the hammer team to reposition
        pre-placed stones to create a more aggressive setup, often aiming for multiple
        points. Despite its strategic importance, evidence on its effect on shot
        selection and end outcomes is limited. Using end-level data from the World
        Mixed Doubles Championships (2016–2025) and Olympic Winter Games (2018, 2022),
        we analyze how Power Play affects hammer-team shot selection and scoring. End
        outcomes are classified as 0, 1, or 2+ points, and shot selection is measured
        as the share of setup shots per end. We examine associations across score
        states and end timing, using a multinomial logit model adjusted for score
        state, end group, and team strength, supplemented by descriptive comparisons.
        Power Play is associated with more offensive shots, lower probability of zero
        points, higher probability of 2+ points, and increased expected scoring,
        especially in late-end comeback situations.
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Introduction}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Mixed Doubles matches last 8 ends; each end starts with one stone per team.
          \item The hammer team can use the Power Play once per game to shift stones for
                scoring advantage.
          \item Power Play aims to increase multipoint scoring and reduce the chance of a
                steal.
        \end{itemize}

        \textbf{Research Questions}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item How does Power Play use affect hammer-team shot selection?
          \item How does Power Play influence end scoring outcomes?
          \item Which match contexts maximize Power Play effectiveness?

        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Data}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Shot-level data from World Championships and Olympics aggregated to end level.
                \cite{ritchie2025opening}
          \item Score state calculated from the hammer team’s perspective.
          \item Outcome: points scored (0 / 1 / 2+); shot selection: share of offensive/setup
                shots
        \end{itemize}

      \begin{block}{Methods}
        We analyzed the \textbf{Power Play (PP)} using two models with
        cluster-robust standard errors by unique match.

        \begin{itemize}
          \item \textbf{Variables:} $x_{PP}$ (Power Play), $x_S$ (Score State), $x_E$ (End group), $x_{str}$ (Strength diff).
          \item \textbf{Interactions:} Account for varying PP effectiveness by score and timing.
        \end{itemize}

        \textbf{1. Shot Selection (Binomial Generalized Linear Model (GLM))}: \\
        Predicting probability $p$ of an offensive shot:
        \[ \text{logit}(p) = \beta_0 + \beta_{1}x_{PP} + \beta_{2}x_{S} + \beta_{3}x_{E} + \beta_{4}(x_{PP}x_{S}) + \beta_{5j}(x_{PP}x_{E}) + \beta_{6j}x_{str} \]

        \textbf{2. Scoring Outcomes (Multinomial Logit)}: \\
        For outcomes $j \in \{1, 2+\}$ relative to a 0-point baseline:
        \[ \ln\left(\frac{\Pr(Y=j)}{\Pr(Y=0)}\right) = \alpha_j + \beta_{1j}x_{PP} + \beta_{2j}x_{S} + \beta_{3j}x_{E} + \beta_{4j}(x_{PP}x_{S}) + \beta_{5j}(x_{PP}x_{E}) + \beta_{6j}x_{str} \]

      \end{block}

        \begin{itemize}
          \item Teams on a power play are much more likely to score 1 point or 2+ points
                compared to trailing teams in early ends.
          \item Interactions show that the impact of power plays is smaller when the team is
                leading or tied.
          \item Stronger teams are significantly more likely to score in any end.
        \end{itemize}

        \begin{itemize}
          \item Late ends see lower probabilities of scoring 2+ without power play, but higher
                probabilities with power play.
          \item Trailing hammer teams in late ends have the highest predicted probability of

        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
      \begin{alertblock}{Discussion}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Teams most commonly use Power Play when leading in late ends.
          \item However, Power Play is most beneficial towards scoring 2 or more points when
                trailing in late ends.
          \item Hammer teams adjust offensive shot rates based on score state and end,
                indicating strategic risk management.
          \item Future research could explore player-level behavior or shot-level sequencing
                for finer-grained insights.

        \end{itemize}

      \end{alertblock}
