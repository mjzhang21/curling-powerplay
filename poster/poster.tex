      \begin{block}{Abstract}

        The Power Play in mixed doubles curling allows the hammer team to reposition
        pre-placed stones to create a more aggressive setup, often aiming for multiple
        points. Despite its strategic importance, evidence on its effect on shot
        selection and end outcomes is limited. Using end-level data from the World
        Mixed Doubles Championships (2016–2025) and Olympic Winter Games (2018, 2022),
        we analyze how Power Play affects hammer-team shot selection and scoring. End
        outcomes are classified as 0, 1, or 2+ points, and shot selection is measured
        as the share of setup shots per end. We examine associations across score
        states and end timing, using a multinomial logit model adjusted for score
        state, end group, and team strength, supplemented by descriptive comparisons.
        Power Play is associated with more offensive shots, lower probability of zero
        points, higher probability of 2+ points, and increased expected scoring,
        especially in late-end comeback situations. Changes in scoring variance vary by
        game state.

      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Introduction}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Mixed Doubles matches last 8 ends; each end starts with one stone per team.
          \item The hammer team can use the Power Play once per game to shift stones for
                scoring advantage.
          \item Power Play aims to increase multi-point scoring and reduce the chance of a
                steal.
        \end{itemize}

        \textbf{Research Questions}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item How does Power Play influence shot selection and scoring?
          \item Which match contexts maximize its advantage?
          \item How does it affect expected points and variance?
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Data}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Shot-level data from World Championships and Olympics aggregated to end level.
          \item Score state calculated from the hammer team’s perspective.
          \item Outcome: points scored (0 / 1 / 2+); shot selection: share of offensive/setup
                shots
        \end{itemize}

      \begin{block}{Methods}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Visualized Power Play usage by end and score state.
          \item Compared expected points and variance of standard and Power Play.
          \item Modeled hammer-team offensive shot proportion (GLM) and end outcomes (ordinal
                logistic regression).
          \item Modeled expected outcomes across game contexts.
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Descriptive Statistics}
        \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{../manuscript/figures/usage.pdf}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Ends 7–8: Power Play used most when leading, least when trailing.
          \item Ends 1–6: Used most when leading, least when tied.
          \item Strategy varies by score and end.
        \end{itemize}

        \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{../manuscript/figures/risk.pdf}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Expected Points increases during all Power Play ends.
          \item Tied Ends show the largest difference in expected points and standard
                deviation.
          \item Leading and Trailing Ends show milder changes in standard deviation.
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}

      \begin{block}{Predicted Probabilites}
        \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{../manuscript/figures/strategy.pdf}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Ends less than 6 show similar, but slightly higher predicted probabilities in
                offensive rate.
          \item Power Play is associated with less offensive shots in leading and tied states
                compared to standard ends.

        \end{itemize}

        \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{../manuscript/figures/outcomes.pdf}
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Compared to standard ends, all score states are associated with higher
                predicted probabilities of scoring 2+.
          \item Trailing ends in ends greater than 7 show largest positive difference in
                scoring 2+ and negative difference in scoring 0.
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
      \begin{alertblock}{Discussion}
        \begin{itemize}

          \item Power Play use varies by match context.
          \item It increases expected points, with variance effects depending on score state.
          \item Shot selection shifts: teams play defensively when leading and offensively when
                trailing.
          \item Late ends see less offensive play but greater scoring impact.
          \item Despite low usage in late trailing ends, it has the largest effect, boosting
                probability of scoring 2+ points.
        \end{itemize}

      \end{alertblock}
