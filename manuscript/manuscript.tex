\title{Power Play Usage and End Outcomes in Elite Mixed Doubles Curling}


\author{
Mark Zhang and Jun Yan \\
Department of Statistics, University of Connecticut
}

\begin{abstract}
        The Power Play rule in mixed doubles curling allows the hammer team to
        reposition the pre-placed stones at the start of an end to create a more
        aggressive scoring configuration, typically intended to produce a multi-point
        end rather than a single point. Despite its strategic importance in elite
        competition, empirical evidence on how Power Play is associated with tactical
        shot selection and end outcomes remains limited. Using end-level data from the
        World Mixed Doubles Curling Championships (2016–2025) and the Olympic Winter
        Games (2018, 2022), we analyze the association between Power Play use,
        hammer-team shot selection, and scoring outcomes from the hammer-team
        perspective. End outcomes are classified as 0 points, 1 point, or 2 or more
        points, and hammer-team shot selection is measured as the share of setup shots
        among classified hammer shots within an end. We first examine how Power Play is
        associated with hammer-team shot selection across score states and end timing.
        We then estimate its association with end outcomes using a multinomial logit
        model adjusting for score state, end group, and relative team strength,
        supplemented by descriptive comparisons of actual hammer-team points scored.
        Power Play is associated with differences in hammer-team shot selection across
        score states. Across game situations, it is associated with a lower probability
        of scoring zero points, a higher probability of scoring two or more points, and
        higher expected hammer-team scoring, with the largest differences observed in
        late-end comeback situations. Changes in scoring dispersion vary by game state.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Curling, often described as ``chess on ice'', has become one of the most widely
followed Winter Olympic sports \citep{agduman2023review}. Rule changes have
played an important role in shaping the sport's competitive and spectator
appeal. For example, the Free Guard Zone was introduced to encourage more
offensive and dynamic play, and subsequent evidence suggests that later
modifications reduced blank ends and increased scoring
\citep{agduman2023review,agduman2023impact}. Mixed doubles curling presents a
distinct strategic setting because its rules differ in several important ways
from traditional team curling, including pre-placed stones, a modified free
guard zone, and the Power Play option \citep{worldcurlingrules2025}. The Power
Play may be used once per match when a team has the hammer and alters the
initial stone configuration to open the center of the sheet. Because this
feature changes the tactical environment of an end, it provides a useful
setting for studying how shot execution and declared tactical choices are
associated with scoring outcomes.

Research on strategy and tactics in curling remains relatively limited.
\citet{zacharias2024examination} identified only eleven studies in this area.
Existing work has focused primarily on strategic questions in traditional
curling, especially the value of the hammer and the decision to blank an end
rather than take a single point. These questions have been examined using
decision analysis, Markov models, and simulation-based approaches
\citep{kostuk2006paradox,willoughby2005analysis, clement2012analysis,
        brenzel2019analysis, fry2024elementary}. Other studies have documented the
importance of last-stone advantage and related performance indicators at the
match level \citep{park2013analysis,erhan2023exploring}. Advances in technology
and digital scorebooks have also improved the availability of detailed shot
data for curling analysis \citep{masui2016informatics}.

Recent work has begun to address mixed doubles more directly.
\citet{ritchie2025opening} introduced a large international mixed doubles
dataset and provided descriptive analyses of shot, end, game, and team-level
patterns. Their results showed that the Power Play is typically used in later
ends, particularly ends 5 through 8, and that teams using the Power Play are
more likely to score two or three points while being less likely to concede a
steal. However, existing work remains largely descriptive and has not directly
examined how score context and Power Play timing are associated with successful
conversion by the hammer team.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

We used the shot-level mixed doubles curling dataset compiled by
\citet{ritchie2025opening}. The dataset was scraped from Curlit Results
Booklets and includes matches from the World Mixed Doubles Curling
Championships (2016--2025) and the Olympic Winter Games (2018 and 2022)
\citep{ritchie2025opening}. It contains 64{,}471 recorded shots and 167 match-,
end-, and stone-level variables. Available variables include match and end
identifiers, end number, hammer designation, team identities, a Power Play
indicator, pre-end and post-end score states, and shot-level measures such as
shot number, declared shot type, stone coordinates, and a five-level execution
score (0, 25, 50, 75, or 100), with higher values indicating better execution.

Our analysis focused on end-level outcomes, so we grouped the shot-level data
down to end-level. To classify the aggression of the hammer team in each end,
we classified the team's Draws, Guards and Raises as offensive and Takeouts and
Clearing shots as defensive. Dividing the number of offensive shots by all
classified shots gives us that team's offensive shot rate. Because Power Play
ends occurred primarily in later stages of matches, end number was grouped into
two categories: $\leq 6$ and $\geq 7$. We also constructed contextual
covariates. Score difference before the end was defined as the hammer team's
score minus the non-hammer team's score at the start of the end and grouped
into three categories: tied, leading, and trailing. Team strength was measured
using a weighted average of hammer, force, and steal efficiencies for each
nation, following \citet{ritchie2025opening}, and strength difference was
defined as the hammer-team value minus the non-hammer-team value.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:meth}

The analysis was conducted at the end level from the perspective of the hammer
team. The primary objective was to assess whether use of the Power Play was
associated with a different scoring profile for the hammer team and whether
that association varied according to game situation. The end outcome was coded
as a three-category variable: 0 points, 1 point, or 2 or more points scored by
the hammer team in the end.

The main explanatory variable was whether the hammer team used the Power Play.
To represent game context, two situational covariates were included: pre-end
score state (hammer team leading, tied, or trailing) and end timing (ends
\(1\)--\(6\) versus ends \(7+\)). Relative team quality was also incorporated
through the difference between hammer-team and non-hammer-team strength,
standardized before modeling.

In addition to scoring outcome, we constructed an end-level measure of declared
tactical style for the hammer team. Using the hammer team's shots, declared
shot types were classified as offensive or defensive, and an offensive shot
rate was calculated as the proportion of classified hammer-team shots that were
offensive. This measure was used to describe tactical orientation across game
states.

Three regression models were estimated. First, a binomial generalized linear
model related the number of offensive hammer-team shots to Power Play use,
score state, end group, their interactions with Power Play, and standardized
strength difference. Second, because the Power Play is a strategic choice
rather than a randomly assigned condition, a logistic regression model was used
to examine how score state and end timing were associated with Power Play use.
Third, the main inferential model was a multinomial logit model for the
three-category end outcome, with 0 points as the reference category. This model
included Power Play use, score state, end group, Power Play interactions with
score state and end group, and standardized strength difference.

Standard errors were clustered at the match level to account for dependence
among ends from the same match. For interpretation of the multinomial model, we
computed predicted probabilities for each combination of Power Play status,
score state, and end group while holding standardized strength difference at
its mean. We then summarized the estimated effect of the Power Play as the
percentage-point difference in predicted probabilities between Power Play and
non-Power-Play ends within the same game state.

When the hammer team was trailing in ends \(1\)--\(6\), Power Play use was
associated with a 14.8 percentage-point decrease in the predicted probability
of scoring 0 points and a 15.6 percentage-point increase in the predicted
probability of scoring \(2+\) points. In ends \(7+\), the corresponding changes
were \(-21.0\) and \(+21.4\) percentage points. When the score was tied, the
estimated increase in the predicted probability of scoring \(2+\) was 10.3
percentage points in ends \(1\)--\(6\) and 15.9 percentage points in ends
\(7+\). When the hammer team was leading, the corresponding increases were 2.8
and 8.7 percentage points.

Overall, the estimated differences were largest in trailing states and in later
ends. The probability of scoring exactly 1 point changed relatively little
compared with the probabilities of scoring 0 or \(2+\) points.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}

This study examined how Power Play use was associated with scoring outcomes in
elite mixed doubles curling across different game situations. Overall, Power
Play use was associated with a lower predicted probability of scoring 0 points
and a higher predicted probability of scoring 2 or more points. These
differences were largest when the hammer team was trailing and in later ends,
indicating that the Power Play is most strongly associated with favorable
offensive outcomes when teams are trying to recover scoreboard disadvantage.

The results also suggest that teams use the Power Play strategically rather
than uniformly. Descriptively, Power Play use was more common when teams were
trailing, especially in ends 7 and later, and these were also the contexts in
which the estimated increase in the probability of scoring 2 or more points was
largest. From a coaching perspective, this supports the practical value of the
Power Play as a comeback tool. When trailing late in the game, teams often need
to create a multiple-point end rather than simply retain hammer advantage, and
the present findings suggest that the Power Play is associated with that type
of higher-reward scoring profile.

A notable feature of the results is that the Power Play effect was driven
mainly by a shift between scoring 0 points and scoring 2 or more points, with
relatively small changes in the probability of scoring exactly 1 point. This
pattern is consistent with the purpose of the rule, which is designed to create
a more favorable offensive setup for the hammer team. For practitioners, this
implies that the Power Play should be judged less by whether a team scores at
all and more by whether it increases the likelihood of producing a multi-point
end compared with the standard stone setup.

The offensive shot-rate summaries were less consistent than the end-outcome
results, suggesting that broad classifications of declared shot type may not
fully capture tactical intent in mixed doubles curling. In practice, effective
Power Play execution may depend not simply on calling more offensive shots, but
on choosing the right sequence of shots to maintain scoring pressure and
favorable stone positioning. This is relevant for coaches, because it suggests
that training for Power Play situations should emphasize end construction and
decision-making across the full sequence of shots rather than isolated shot
categories alone.

These findings have practical implications for high-performance preparation.
Teams may benefit from rehearsing Power Play decision-making under specific
score and timing scenarios, particularly when trailing late in matches.
Scenario-based training may be especially useful for improving communication,
shot selection, and execution under pressure in ends where a multiple-point
score is needed.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. This was an observational study, so
the estimated associations should not be interpreted as strictly causal. Teams
choose whether to use the Power Play based on factors that may not be fully
captured in the available data. In addition, the sample consists of elite
international competition, so the findings may not generalize directly to other
levels of play. Future work could link Power Play use to match-winning
probability and incorporate more detailed shot-sequence or spatial information
to better identify how successful Power Play ends are constructed.

In summary, Power Play use in elite mixed doubles curling was associated with a
more favorable hammer-team scoring profile, particularly when teams were
trailing and in later ends. For coaches and performance analysts, these
findings support the Power Play as a context-dependent offensive tool with the
greatest apparent value in high-leverage comeback situations.
