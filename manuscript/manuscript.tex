\title{Associations of Early Hammer-Shot Execution and Shot Type With Power Play Success in Elite Mixed Doubles Curling}

\author{
Mark Zhang and Jun Yan \\
Department of Statistics, University of Connecticut
}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
        The Power Play in mixed doubles curling is designed to create multipoint
        scoring opportunities, yet quantitative evidence on how teams convert these
        ends remains limited. Using shot-level data from World Mixed Doubles Curling
        Championships (2016--2025) and the Olympic Winter Games (2018, 2022), we
        provide a focused empirical analysis of how hammer-team execution and declared
        shot types on shots 2, 4, and 6 are associated with scoring two or more
        points. In a multivariable logistic regression adjusting for end timing,
        pre-end score difference, and relative team strength, higher execution on all
        three shots was associated with higher odds of success, especially on shot 6.
        Shot-call associations were smaller and varied by shot number. As a focused
        shot-level study of Power Play conversion in elite mixed doubles curling, these
        findings provide baseline empirical evidence suggesting that execution quality
        is more consistently associated with success than declared shot type.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Curling, often described as ``chess on ice,'' has become one of the most widely
followed Winter Olympic sports \citep{agduman2023review}. Rule changes have
played an important role in shaping the sport's competitive and spectator
appeal. For example, the Free Guard Zone was introduced to encourage more
offensive and dynamic play, and subsequent evidence suggests that later
modifications reduced blank ends and increased scoring
\citep{agduman2023review,agduman2023impact}. Mixed doubles curling presents a
distinct strategic setting because its rules differ in several important ways
from traditional team curling, including pre-placed stones, a modified free
guard zone, and the Power Play option \citep{worldcurlingrules2025}. The Power
Play may be used once per match when a team has the hammer and alters the
initial stone configuration to open the center of the sheet. Because this
feature changes the tactical environment of an end, it provides a useful
setting for studying how shot execution and declared tactical choices are
associated with scoring outcomes.

Research on strategy and tactics in curling remains relatively limited.
\citet{zacharias2024examination} identified only eleven studies in this area.
Existing work has focused primarily on strategic questions in traditional
curling, especially the value of the hammer and the decision to blank an end
rather than take a single point. These questions have been examined using
decision analysis, Markov models, and simulation-based approaches
\citep{kostuk2006paradox,willoughby2005analysis,clement2012analysis,brenzel2019analysis,fry2024elementary}.
Other studies have documented the importance of last-stone advantage and
related performance indicators at the match level
\citep{park2013analysis,erhan2023exploring}. Advances in technology and digital
scorebooks have also improved the availability of detailed shot data for
curling analysis \citep{masui2016informatics}.

Recent work has begun to address mixed doubles more directly.
\citet{ritchie2025opening} introduced a large international mixed doubles
dataset and provided descriptive analyses of shot, end, game, and team-level
patterns. Their results showed that the Power Play is typically used in later
ends, particularly ends 5 through 8, and that teams using the Power Play are
more likely to score two or three points while being less likely to concede a
steal. However, existing work remains largely descriptive and has not directly
examined which shot-level features within Power Play ends are associated with
successful conversion by the hammer team. In particular, prior work has not
focused on whether execution quality and declared throw type on the early
hammer shots are associated with scoring two or more points in a Power Play
end.

Existing mixed doubles research has shown when teams tend to use the Power Play
and how outcomes differ descriptively across end types, but less is known about
which shot-level factors within Power Play ends are associated with successful
conversion by the hammer team. In particular, prior work has not separated the
roles of execution quality and declared tactical choice on the early hammer
shots. Using international elite mixed doubles data, this study provides a
focused observational analysis of how execution and throw type on hammer shots
2, 4, and 6 are associated with scoring two or more points in a Power Play end.
In doing so, we offer a shot-level empirical benchmark for Power Play
conversion in an understudied tactical setting.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

We used the shot-level mixed doubles curling dataset compiled by
\citet{ritchie2025opening}. The dataset was scraped from Curlit Results
Booklets and includes matches from the World Mixed Doubles Curling
Championships (2016--2025) and the Olympic Winter Games (2018 and 2022)
\citep{ritchie2025opening}. It contains 64{,}471 recorded shots and 167 match-,
end-, and stone-level variables. Available variables include match and end
identifiers, end number, hammer designation, team identities, a Power Play
indicator, pre-end and post-end score states, and shot-level measures such as
shot number, declared shot type, stone coordinates, and a five-level execution
score (0, 25, 50, 75, or 100), with higher values indicating better execution.

Our analysis focused on the first three hammer shots in Power Play ends,
corresponding to hammer shots 2, 4, and 6. We focused on hammer shots 2, 4, and
6 because they represent the hammer team’s first three deliveries within the
end and therefore capture the early tactical construction of a Power Play
before the final stones. These shots provide a natural window for examining
whether execution quality and declared shot type are associated with later end
success. To ensure comparability across observations, we restricted the sample
to non-conceded ends with a complete shot structure and then filtered to Power
Play ends only. Hammer-team and non-hammer-team variables were defined by
aligning team identities with the hammer designation so that all score states
and covariates were expressed from the hammer team's perspective. This yielded
an analytic sample of 1289 Power Play ends.

To improve interpretability and reduce sparsity, declared shot types were
collapsed into four throw groups: Draw (draw, freeze), Guard (guard, front),
Peel/Raise (wick-soft-peeling, raise), and Removal (take-out, double take-out,
hit-and-roll, promotion take-out, clearing). Because Power Play ends occurred
primarily in later stages of matches, end number was grouped into five
categories: $\leq 4$, 5, 6, 7, and 8. We also constructed contextual
covariates. Score difference before the end was defined as the hammer team's
score minus the non-hammer team's score at the start of the end. Team strength
was measured using a weighted average of hammer, force, and steal efficiencies
for each nation, following \citet{ritchie2025opening}, and strength difference
was defined as the hammer-team value minus the non-hammer-team value.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:meth}

The primary outcome was hammer-end success, defined as the hammer team scoring
two or more points in the end. Associations with hammer-end success were
estimated using logistic regression. Predictors included shot-specific
execution score, shot-specific throw group, end group, pre-end score
difference, and strength difference.

To represent the first three hammer shots separately, execution score and throw
group were reshaped into shot-specific covariates for hammer shots 2, 4, and 6.
For throw group, Draw served as the reference category for each shot. End 6 was
used as the reference category for end group.

For interpretation, execution scores were scaled by 25 so that a one-unit
change corresponded to a 25-point increase in execution score. Pre-end score
difference was mean-centered, and strength difference was standardized.
Standard errors were clustered at the match level to account for within-match
dependence. Results are reported as odds ratios with 95\% confidence intervals.
Regression analyses used complete-case estimation, excluding ends with missing
values in the outcome or any model covariate. All findings are interpreted as
observational rather than causal, because Power Play timing and shot selection
are strategic decisions that may reflect unmeasured game context and opponent
responses.

\section{Results}\label{sec:resu}

44.1\% of ends (Table~\ref{tab:descriptive_stats}). Shot-call distributions
varied by shot number. Shot 2 was most often a Draw, whereas shots 4 and 6 were
more frequently Removal calls. Power Plays were also concentrated in later
ends, especially ends 6 and 7.

Among shot-call variables, a Guard call on shot 2 was associated with lower
odds of success relative to Draw (OR $= 0.54$, 95\% CI $[0.35,\,0.83]$,
$p=0.005$), whereas a Guard call on shot 6 was associated with higher odds of
success (OR $= 2.35$, 95\% CI $[1.08,\,5.14]$, $p=0.032$). In addition, success
odds were lower in ends $\leq 4$ and in end 8 relative to end 6, and greater
relative team strength was associated with higher odds of success.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
As a focused shot-level empirical study of Power Play conversion, our analysis
highlights several patterns in how shot type, execution quality, and match
context are associated with whether the hammer team scores two or more points
in a Power Play end.

First, hammer-end success was most strongly associated with shot execution,
supporting the view that mixed doubles is a low-margin format that rewards
precise shot-making. Although shot 6 execution showed the largest association,
execution on shots 2 and 4 was also positively associated with success. Taken
together, these results suggest that Power Play conversion is more closely
associated with consistent execution across early hammer shots than with any
single throw sequence.

Second, shot-call categories showed different associations across shot numbers.
On shot 2, Guard calls were associated with lower odds of success relative to
Draw, whereas on shot 6, Guard calls were associated with higher odds of
success. These differences likely reflect that declared shot type is partly a
response to the evolving board state and strategic context of the end.
Accordingly, the estimated associations for throw group should be interpreted
cautiously and not as causal effects of tactical choice alone.

These interpretations are limited by the information available in the model.
Throw types are selected in response to score differential, stone placement,
and opponent actions, so the estimated shot-call associations likely reflect
unmeasured end-state information in addition to tactical intent. Incorporating
spatial coordinates and more detailed board-state features would allow a more
complete analysis of positioning and tactical choice. In addition, modeling the
full end score rather than a binary $\geq 2$ outcome could provide a fuller
picture of Power Play performance.

Match context was also associated with Power Play conversion. Lower success
odds in early ends and in end 8 suggest that timing is related to both
strategic choice and the likelihood of scoring two or more points. Power Plays
used in early and late ends may sometimes serve a more defensive purpose, such
as limiting the risk of a high-scoring end. The association with relative team
strength further suggests that stronger teams are more likely to translate
open-sheet conditions into multipoint scores, even after adjustment for
shot-level variables.

Overall, these findings suggest that execution quality across the first three
hammer shots is more consistently associated with Power Play conversion than
declared shot type. This work provides a focused empirical benchmark for an
understudied strategic setting in elite mixed doubles curling. Future work that
incorporates spatial measures of board state and models the full end score
could build on these results to provide more situation-specific tactical
guidance.
