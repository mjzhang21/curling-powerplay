\title{Associations of Early Hammer-Shot Execution and Shot Type With Power Play Success in Elite Mixed Doubles Curling}

\author{
Mark Zhang and Jun Yan \\
Department of Statistics, University of Connecticut
}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

The Power Play in mixed doubles curling is designed to create multipoint
scoring opportunities, yet quantitative evidence on how teams convert these
ends remains limited. Using shot-level data from World Mixed Doubles Curling
Championships (2016--2025) and the Olympic Winter Games (2018, 2022), we
provide an initial empirical analysis of how hammer-team execution and declared
shot types on shots 2, 4, and 6 are associated with scoring two or more
points.In a multivariable logistic regression adjusting for end timing, pre-end
score difference, and relative team strength, higher execution on all three
shots was associated with higher odds of success, especially on shot 6.
Shot-call associations were smaller and varied by shot number. As an initial
shot-level study of Power Play conversion in elite mixed doubles curling, these
findings provide baseline empirical evidence suggesting that execution quality
is more consistently associated with success than declared shot type.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Curling, often described as ``chess on ice,'' has become one of the most widely
followed Winter Olympic sports \citep{agduman2023review}. With increased
international attention, the World Curling Federation introduced the Free Guard
Zone to make matches more competitive and exciting to watch
\citep{agduman2023review}. Rule changes can meaningfully affect tactical and
strategic decisions; for example, \citet{agduman2023impact} found that the Free
Guard Zone reduced blanks and increased scoring. Mixed doubles curling presents
a distinct strategic setting, with features such as the modified free guard
zone, pre-placed stones, and the Power Play altering incentives and increasing
the importance of adaptive planning \citep{worldcurlingrules2025}. Developed at
the request of the International Olympic Committee as a faster and more
variable format, mixed doubles has received relatively little data-driven
strategic study. The Power Play, a key innovation, may be used once per match
when a team has the hammer and opens the center of the sheet by repositioning
the pre-placed stones. This rule change creates a setting in which clearer
evidence on shot selection and execution may be useful.

Research on strategic and tactical factors in curling remains limited, with
\citet{zacharias2024examination} identifying only eleven studies on the topic.
Advances in technology and digital scorebooks have improved the collection of
detailed match data, allowing analysts to study links between shot accuracy and
game outcomes \citep{masui2016informatics}. One longstanding question is
whether the hammer team benefits more from blanking an end or taking a single
point \citep{kostuk2006paradox}. Prior studies have used decision analysis and
Markov chains to evaluate win probabilities in these scenarios
\citep{willoughby2005analysis,clement2012analysis}. More recent work has
extended these approaches through simulation and by examining conceded ends
\citep{brenzel2019analysis,fry2024elementary}. In addition, regression-based
studies have documented the advantage of possessing the hammer
\citep{park2013analysis,erhan2023exploring}. However, these studies do not
address the strategic setting created by mixed doubles rule changes,
particularly the Power Play, and they do not examine shot-level measures such
as execution score and declared throw type. To our knowledge, prior curling
analytics work has not provided a shot-level empirical analysis of Power Play
conversion in elite mixed doubles. Because the Power Play changes incentives
and opens distinct scoring opportunities, an initial empirical description of
the factors associated with successful conversion is needed.

In this study, we use international mixed doubles shot-level data to provide an
initial empirical analysis of Power Play ends and to estimate associations
between execution quality, tactical choices on the first three hammer shots,
and hammer-end success, defined as scoring two or more points in the end. Using
shot-level execution scores and declared throw types, we document baseline
patterns linking these factors to end-level outcomes. Our aim is to provide a
first empirical benchmark for Power Play conversion in elite mixed doubles
while interpreting all results as observational rather than causal.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

We used the shot-level dataset compiled and processed by
\citet{ritchie2025opening}. The data were scraped from Curlit Results Booklets
and include World Mixed Doubles Curling Championship matches from 2016 to 2025
and 2018 and 2022 Olympic Winter Games \citep{ritchie2025opening}. The dataset
contains 64{,}471 recorded shots and 167 match-, end-, and stone-level
variables. Key variables include match and end identifiers, end number, hammer
designation, team identities, a Power Play indicator, pre-/during-/post-end
scoring states, and shot-level measures such as shot number, coordinates,
declared shot type, and a five-level execution score (0, 25, 50, 75, 100),
where higher values indicate better execution.

We focused on the first three hammer shots in Power Play ends. To ensure
comparability across ends, we restricted the sample to ends with a complete
shot structure and excluded conceded ends. We then filtered the dataset to
Power Play ends and to hammer team shots 2, 4, and 6. Hammer and non-hammer
team variables were created by matching the hammer indicator to team identities
so that score states and covariates were consistently defined from the hammer
team's perspective.

To improve interpretability and avoid sparse categories, we grouped declared
shot types into four throw groups. Draws and freezes were classified as Draw;
guards and fronts as Guard; wick-soft-peeling and raise as Peel/Raise; and
take-out, double take-out, hit-and-roll, promotion take-out, and clearing as
Removal. Because Power Play ends occur disproportionately in later ends, we
binned end number into five groups: $\le4$, 5, 6, 7, and 8. We also constructed
contextual covariates. Score difference before the end was defined as the
hammer team's score minus the non-hammer team's score at the start of the end.
Team strength was calculated using a weighted average of hammer, force, and
steal efficiencies computed across all ends played by each nation
\citep{ritchie2025opening}, and strength difference was defined as hammer-team
strength minus non-hammer-team strength.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:meth}

The primary outcome was hammer-end success, defined as the hammer team scoring
two or more points in the end. We estimated associations using logistic
regression with hammer-end success as the binary outcome and shot-specific
execution, shot-specific throw group, end group, pre-end score difference, and
strength difference as predictors.

To estimate shot-specific associations, execution score and throw group were
pivoted to create separate covariates for hammer shots 2, 4, and 6 (e.g.,
shot-specific execution and throw-group indicators). For each shot, Draw was
used as the reference throw group, and end 6 was used as the reference end
group.

For modeling, execution scores were scaled by 25 (so one unit corresponds to a
25-point increase), score difference was mean-centered, and strength difference
was standardized. Standard errors were clustered at the match level to account
for within-match dependence, and results are reported as odds ratios with 95\%
confidence intervals. We used complete-case analysis for the regression,
excluding ends with missing values in the outcome or any model covariate. All
associations are observational; Power Play timing and shot selection are
strategic decisions and may reflect unmeasured end state and opponent
responses.

varied by shot number. Shot 2 was most often a Draw, whereas shots 4 and 6 were
more frequently Removal calls. Power Plays were also concentrated in later
ends, especially ends 6 and 7.

Among shot-call variables, a Guard call on shot 2 was associated with lower
odds of success relative to Draw (OR $= 0.54$, 95\% CI $[0.35,\,0.83]$,
$p=0.005$), whereas a Guard call on shot 6 was associated with higher odds of
success (OR $= 2.35$, 95\% CI $[1.08,\,5.14]$, $p=0.032$). In addition, success
odds were lower in ends $\leq 4$ and in end 8 relative to end 6, and greater
relative team strength was associated with higher odds of success.

As a secondary analysis, we evaluated out-of-sample predictive performance
using grouped 5-fold cross-validation at the match level. The model showed
moderate discrimination for Power Play success, with mean test AUC of 0.691 (SD
0.015), and reasonable probabilistic accuracy, with mean Brier score of 0.221
(SD 0.004). Mean classification accuracy at a 0.5 threshold was 0.634 (SD
0.010). These results suggest that early hammer-shot execution, shot type, and
match context contain useful but incomplete information about end-level
success.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
As one of the first shot-level empirical studies of Power Play conversion in
elite mixed doubles curling, our analysis highlights several patterns in how
shot type, execution quality, and match context are associated with whether the
hammer team scores two or more points in a Power Play end. First, hammer-end
success was most strongly associated with shot execution, supporting the view
that mixed doubles is a low-margin format that rewards precise shot-making.
Although shot 6 execution showed the largest association, execution on shots 2
and 4 was also positively associated with hammer-end success. Taken together,
these results suggest that Power Play success is more closely associated with
consistent shot quality than with a single prescribed early-end throw sequence.

Second, shot-call categories appeared to have different associations depending
on shot number. Early in the end (shot 2), call choices are tied to
establishing scoring structure under the shifted pre-placed stones. In this
setting, Draw-based development may be more compatible with immediate scoring
pressure, whereas early Guard usage may reflect more conservative planning or a
response to an unfavorable opening position. Mid-end (shot 4), Peel/Raise
attempts may represent higher-variance efforts to reshape the scoring layout
when simpler options are less available. Late in the end (shot 6), the call
label may provide less incremental information once the end state is largely
determined, and the outcome may be more closely related to how precisely the
chosen plan is executed.

These interpretations are limited by the information available in the model.
Throw types are selected in response to the evolving score differential and
board position, so the estimated shot-call associations should not be
interpreted as causal effects of tactical choice alone. Incorporating spatial
coordinates would allow for a more detailed analysis of board positioning in
relation to declared throw type. In addition, modeling the full end score
rather than a binary $\geq 2$ outcome may provide a fuller picture of outcome
variation.

Match context was also associated with Power Play conversion. Lower success
odds in early ends and in end 8 suggest that timing is related to both
strategic choice and the likelihood of scoring two or more points. Power Plays
used in early and late ends may sometimes serve a more defensive purpose, such
as limiting the risk of a high-scoring end. The association with relative team
strength further suggests that stronger teams are more likely to translate
open-sheet conditions into multipoint scores, even after adjustment for
shot-level variables.

Overall, these findings suggest that execution quality across the first three
hammer shots is more consistently associated with Power Play conversion than
declared shot type. As an initial shot-level study of Power Play ends in elite
mixed doubles curling, this work provides baseline empirical evidence for an
understudied strategic setting. Future work that incorporates spatial measures
of board state and models the full end score could build on these results to
provide more situation-specific tactical guidance.
